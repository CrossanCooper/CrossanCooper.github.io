Working Papers

  1. "Where Does the Tide Roll? The Causes and Consequences of Out-of-State Enrollment at Public Flagships"

    with Ryan Haygood | Presented at AEFP '26 | Draft available upon request

    Abstract: Public flagship universities increasingly use nonresident enrollment to raise tuition revenue. National data show that out-of-state enrollment shares have risen sharply at public flagships and that lower state support is associated with higher application and attendance rates among high-income nonresidents. To examine how universities pursue this revenue margin and how it affects graduate migration and state budgets, we link two decades of University of Alabama (UA) commencement records to admissions office recruiting visits and individual employment histories. UA admissions officers target large, high-income out-of-state markets with strong prior demand, and visited towns produce 8-10% more future UA graduates in models with town and school-year fixed effects. Nonresident enrollment growth does not reduce in-state degree production in levels but does redirect resident graduates across state labor markets. To capture these spillovers, we estimate a location choice model that exploits variation in the state-level composition of UA cohorts; our estimates imply that a 1 percentage point increase in a cohort's out-of-state share raises in-state out-migration by 0.33 percentage points. A counterfactual holding nonresident shares at their 2006 levels suggests that 2,719 additional resident graduates left Alabama because of rising nonresident enrollment. A state-budget accounting exercise shows that this migration response is costly but not large enough to offset nonresident tuition margins. Under baseline assumptions, an additional out-of-state student generates $12,516 in lifetime state budget surplus.
