Working Papers

  1. "Where Does the Tide Roll? The Causes and Consequences of Out-of-State Enrollment at Public Flagships"

    with Ryan Haygood | Presented at AEFP '26 | Draft available upon request

    Abstract: Public flagship universities increasingly use nonresident tuition revenues to offset declining state funding. Nationally, we find that public flagships facing lower state support enroll relatively more high-income nonresidents. To understand how universities pursue this revenue margin and its downstream effects on graduates and state budgets, we link two decades of University of Alabama (UA) commencement records to admissions office recruiting visits and individual employment histories. We show UA boosts high-income out-of-state demand through targeted recruitment visits: two-way fixed-effects models show new visits generate 8–10% more future UA graduates. We then trace how nonresident enrollment redirects graduates across state labor markets. Exploiting variation in the state-of-origin composition of UA cohorts and a shift-share instrument, we estimate that every 100 out-of-state students cause 9–15 additional in-state students to leave the state after college. The resulting fiscal losses to Alabama are outweighed by tax revenues from out-of-state stayers and non-resident tuition margins: altogether, the median out-of-state student generates more than $40,000 in lifetime state budget surplus. Finally, the out-of-state expansion appears to support in-state degree production. A budget accounting exercise shows that UA’s out-of-state enrollment margins are sufficient to cross-subsidize more than half of the in-state cohort.

  1. "Housing Assistance and Human Capital"

    Abstract: This paper studies the impact of housing assistance on children’s human capital accumulation. By reducing exposure to homelessness and other disruptive shocks, housing assistance may improve children’s educational trajectories regardless of impacts on test scores. I test this idea by linking applications to public housing and housing voucher waiting lists in New Haven, Connecticut, to statewide education and criminal justice records. I use the timing of assistance offers generated by waiting list position lotteries as an instrument for children’s exposure to assistance. Receiving assistance reduces school-reported homelessness and housing instability by 3.5 percentage points. These gains in housing stability do not translate into detectable improvements in kindergarten readiness or standardized test scores in grades 3-8; for test scores, I rule out average effects larger than 0.05 standard deviations. In sharp contrast, an additional year of assistance increases high school graduation by 3.8 percentage points and college enrollment by 2.5 percentage points, with suggestive reductions in early-adult felony convictions and increases in employment. Assistance does not increase exposure to higher-quality schools and appears to move children toward schools with lower-achieving and higher-poverty peers. These results suggest that housing assistance improves long-run outcomes by reducing downside risk in the process of human capital accumulation. Mapping the educational attainment effects into predicted impacts on earnings, I estimate that each dollar spent on housing assistance yields $2 in long-run benefits.
